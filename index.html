🏗️ The Layer System

A pavement is a layered structure that sits on top of natural soil (the subgrade). Its whole job is to take a concentrated truck load and spread it wide enough that the soil below doesn't deform permanently.

Think of it like stacking pancakes — each layer spreads the load wider so the ground underneath barely feels the truck above.

Lecture 1 — Traffic Loading & Pavement Distress
🚛 Traffic Loading — ESALs

Cars barely damage pavements. A single heavy truck does as much damage as thousands of cars. So instead of counting every vehicle, we convert all traffic into Equivalent Single Axle Loads (ESALs) — the number of standard 80 kN axle passes that cause the equivalent damage.

🔴 Standard axle = 80 kN (18,000 lb), Single Axle, Dual Tyre (SADT). This is the reference everything is compared against.
The Fourth Power Law

Damage doesn't scale linearly with load — it scales with the 4th power. Double the axle load = 16× the damage.

LEF = (Axle Load / Standard Axle Load)⁴
LEFLoad Equivalency Factor — how damaging this axle is compared to the standard 80 kN axle
Axle LoadThe actual load on the axle being assessed (kN)
Standard Axle80 kN (the reference standard axle)
Power of 4This is the key — damage is NOT linear with load. It's exponential (4th power).
Example: An axle carrying 160 kN (double the standard): LEF = (160/80)⁴ = 2⁴ = 16. That one axle does 16× more damage than the standard axle.

Lecture 2 — Bitumen

Bitumen (also called asphalt binder in North America) is the black sticky stuff that holds aggregates together. It's a viscoelastic material — it behaves like an elastic solid at cold/high-frequency loading but like a viscous fluid at hot/slow loading.

🔴 This dual behaviour is why temperature and speed both matter. Get this concept solid — it underpins half the course.
🌡️ Temperature Sensitivity

Bitumen's behaviour changes dramatically with temperature:

TemperatureBitumen behaviourRisk
High (summer)Soft, viscous, flowsRutting, shoving
IntermediateElastic/flexibleFatigue cracking
Low (winter)Stiff, brittleThermal/low-temp cracking
📊 The Performance Grade (PG) System

The Superpave PG grading system specifies bitumen by the temperature range it can handle. The grade tells you the maximum pavement temperature it can resist without rutting, and the minimum temperature before it cracks.

PG XX - YY
XXMaximum 7-day average pavement temperature (°C) the binder can handle without excessive rutting
YYMinimum pavement design temperature (°C, always negative) before the binder becomes too brittle and cracks
Example: PG 64-22 = binder works up to 64°C (no rutting) and down to -22°C (no thermal cracking). Standard for most NZ/Aus highways at 90 kph.
Bumping Up the Grade for Slow Traffic

Slow or stationary traffic causes more creep, so you need a stiffer binder (higher PG high-temp grade):

LocationWhyPG grade bump
Highway at 90 kphFast loading, short contact timePG 64-22 (standard)
Toll booth areaVehicles slow significantlyPG 70-22 (+1 grade)
Weigh stationVehicles nearly stoppedPG 76-22 (+2 grades)
🔴 Also bump the high-temp grade for high traffic volumes: 10–30 × 10⁶ ESALs → +1 grade. >30 × 10⁶ ESALs → +2 grades.

Lecture 3 — Superpave Aggregates

Aggregates make up about 95% of HMA by weight and about 85% by volume. Getting aggregate properties right is critical — they control mix stiffness, durability, and resistance to both rutting and fatigue.

📐 Aggregate Gradation — The Superpave Restricted Zone

Gradation = the size distribution of aggregate particles. Superpave controls this with a control sieve system — the gradation curve must stay within certain bands.

🔴 The key concept: Superpave introduced a "restricted zone" above the maximum density line on the gradation chart. Mixes that pass THROUGH this zone have too many fine sands and are prone to rutting. Avoid humping through the restricted zone.
💡 NMAS = Nominal Maximum Aggregate Size = one sieve size larger than the first sieve retaining more than 10% of material. This controls the mix type (e.g., 12.5mm NMAS wearing course).
🔍 Aggregate Quality Tests — Superpave Criteria

These tests assess whether aggregates are strong and angular enough for the traffic level. All have minimum/maximum values that depend on traffic (ESALs) and layer position (surface vs. lower).

This is one of the most calculation-heavy topics. Volumetric properties describe the proportions of air, aggregate, and binder in a compacted asphalt mix. Getting the volumes right is critical for durability and performance.

📐 Key Volumetric Terms & Equations
🔴 These equations come up repeatedly in every design calculation. Learn each variable individually.
1. Voids in Mineral Aggregate (VMA)
VMA = 100 - (Gmb × Ps) / Gsb
VMA (%)Voids in Mineral Aggregate — total void space between aggregate particles (includes air voids AND binder space). Must exceed minimum (typically 13–15% depending on NMAS)
GmbBulk specific gravity of the compacted mix — measured in lab on the actual specimen
Ps (%)Aggregate content by total mix mass (as a decimal in some forms, or %)
GsbBulk specific gravity of the aggregate blend
2. Voids Filled with Asphalt (VFA)
VFA = 100 × (VMA - Va) / VMA
VFA (%)Percentage of VMA that is filled with binder (not air). Target: 65–75%
VMA (%)Total voids in mineral aggregate (from above)
Va (%)Air void content in compacted mix. Superpave design target: 4.0% at Ndesign
3. Air Voids (Va)
Va = 100 × (Gmm - Gmb) / Gmm
Va (%)Air void content — the percentage of the mix that is air. Too high = permeable (water/air damage). Too low = unstable (rutting).
GmmMaximum theoretical specific gravity (Rice gravity) — specific gravity of the mix with ZERO air voids. Measured in lab (ASTM D2041).
GmbBulk specific gravity of the compacted specimen — actual density including air voids.
🔴 The gap between Gmm and Gmb IS the air voids. If Gmb = Gmm, you have zero air voids (over-compacted, unstable). If Gmb is much less than Gmm, you have too much air (permeable, weak).
4. Effective Binder Content (Vbe & Pbe)
Pbe = Pb - (Pba × Ps / 100)
Pbe (%)Effective binder content — the binder NOT absorbed into aggregate pores. This is what coats and fills voids.
Pb (%)Total binder content (% of total mix mass)
Pba (%)Absorbed binder content — binder that gets sucked into aggregate pores and does nothing useful
Ps (%)Aggregate content (% of total mix mass)
5. %Gmm — Compaction Check
%Gmm = (Gmb / Gmm) × 100
%Gmm at NiniMust be ≤ 89% — if too high, mix compacts too easily in the field = unstable
%Gmm at NdesignMust equal 96% (= Va of 4%)
%Gmm at NmaxMust be ≥ 98% — ensures mix won't over-compact in service
🔴 The three gyration levels (Nini, Ndesign, Nmax) test if the mix compacts too easily early (Nini), hits 4% air at design level (Ndesign), and doesn't over-compact in service (Nmax).
